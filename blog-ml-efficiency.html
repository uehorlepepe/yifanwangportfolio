Why Perfect Data is the Enemy of Realistic Machine Learning

April 13, 2026 3 min read

While making my model for my research paper, “A Supervised Machine Learning Approach to Identifying Efficiency Gaps in Commercial Aviation,” my model performance was insane. We are talking an R² value of 0.9998. At first, I thought that this was a good thing. However, I had a gut feeling that there was something extremely wrong about the results.

The goal of this research paper was to evaluate how well the model fits and predicts patterns of fuel consumption of all the major commercial aircraft types flying across the world, as well as potential fixes to fuel efficiency.

"The problem was that the synthetic data was too clean, and there was no randomness!"

In real life, planes may take longer to taxi due to delays, therefore causing extra fuel burn. This is something that is not accounted for in my synthetic data as well as my code. This made my results unrealistic and inflated performance metrics.

So, what I realised was that an extremely high R² value was not good. The overfitting caused misleading performance due to the perfect data. And so, the issue wasn’t the model, but the data generation process.

Just a few days ago, my friend was developing a dice game called YanFarkle, where he coded it so that 6 dice would randomly land on numbers from 1-6. That made me think about randomness, and it prompted me to introduce randomness, or noise, into data generation. This made the data less perfect and more realistic.

Now, I am considering adding a slider variable for the noise level, so I can control how messy the data is. Now, the R² value dropped, but remained realistic, at 0.87. Even though it dropped, this now makes our model performance more meaningful, and the results now reflect real-world uncertainty better.

Main Takeaway

Perfect data hides problems, and real-world systems need noise to be properly tested. Better evaluation comes from realism, not ideal conditions.

"A model that only works in perfect conditions isn't really being tested."
